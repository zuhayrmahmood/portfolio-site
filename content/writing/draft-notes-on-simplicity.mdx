Every project I've worked on has a moment where it wants to get complicated. A new
feature, an edge case, a clever abstraction — each one reasonable on its own. Simplicity
is the discipline of noticing that moment and asking whether the complexity is really
earning its keep.

## Simple is a decision, not a default

Left alone, systems accumulate. The default state of any codebase is *more*: more
files, more options, more indirection. Staying simple takes active effort — you have
to keep choosing it.

A few questions I try to ask:

1. Can this be one thing instead of two?
2. Would a future reader guess how it works without asking me?
3. If I deleted it, what would actually break?

## Fewer moving parts

The most reliable code is the code that isn't there. Every dependency, config flag,
and abstraction is a thing that can drift, break, or surprise you later. That doesn't
mean *never* add them — it means the bar should be high.

> Perfection is achieved not when there is nothing more to add, but when there is
> nothing left to take away.

That line gets quoted to death, but it's still true. The hard part isn't knowing it —
it's doing it when the pressure is to just ship the clever version.

## The payoff

Simple things are easier to change, and change is where most of a project's life is
spent. The upfront restraint pays for itself the first time you come back six months
later and still understand what you wrote.
